Setting up

  1. Place the module onto a flat area and make sure it is connected to the battery back and powered. Ensure that at this stage you are connected with your PC to the module via bluetooth.

  2. Place your sample flask onto the module.

  3. Gently drop the mirror module into the flask, ensuring the mirror side faces down. The mirror should move itself to a desirable position based on the magnetic attraction from the base. Now using the provided application (included USB drive with product), press "Callibration" to initiate callibration.

  4. Once callibration is completed, gently move the setup into an incubator.

Setting parameters and recording data

  1. In the application, set an OD600 value at which ou would like to receive your warning. The default is set at OD600 of 0.6.

  2. Press "Start Recording" in the application, ensuring your PC volume is audible and not muted.

  3. Now you can start the incubator, however make sure the flask is safely supported, and the module does not move in relation to the flask.

  4. When the preset OD600 is achieved, your PC will notify you with a buzzer, at which point you can add your reagent into the bacteria culture.

  5. If you would like to view the data recorded by the module, click "View Data" to generate a OD600 vs. time graph of all recorded data.

From idea to prototype

Initial concept design

Our initial concept design started from this sketch. We started from a basic concept which measured Optical Density (OD) by some means, thereby informing the operator at the optimal point to inject a reagent. It was key to make it modular which does not require external power, thereby making it possible to be inserted into an incubator.

Creating a realistic idea

Upon attempting to recreate multiple design iterations, we concluded that this structure was the most realistic, which uses a 660nm laser to shine a ray into the flask, upon which it is reflected by a mirror and bounced back to a photodiode in the module.


Professionals such as postgraduates and lecturers were consulted for a realistic price point and desired functionalities and priorities.

Prototyping with actual circuitry

Our next steps were to reproduce our concept with actual cicruitry. This stage was a key in planning a reasonable footprint for the module, and making sure the circuitry does not get damaged by accidental spills.


From this point forward, significant prototyping was undergone to obtain the optimal shape whilst maintaining accuracy and precision in measurements.
